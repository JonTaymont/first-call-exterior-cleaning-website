The Cape Coral Homeowner's Guide to Paver Cleaning & Sealing

If you own a home in Cape Coral, there's a good chance you have pavers somewhere on your property. Driveways, walkways, pool decks, patios, lanai floors — pavers are everywhere here, and for good reason. They look great, they're durable, and they handle Florida's weather better than poured concrete in most applications.

But pavers aren't maintenance-free. I've worked on hundreds of paver projects around Cape Coral — from brand-new driveways in Sandoval to 15-year-old pool decks in Cape Harbour — and the pattern is always the same. Unsealed pavers fade, grow weeds, attract ants, and lose their joint sand within a few years. Sealed pavers hold their color, resist staining, stay weed-free, and look sharp for 2–3 years between applications.

Here's everything you need to know about the process.

When to seal your pavers

New pavers should be sealed after they've had about 30 days to settle and any efflorescence (that white hazy film on new concrete products) has had time to work its way out. Sealing too early traps efflorescence under the sealant, which looks terrible.

Existing pavers should be re-sealed when: — The color has faded noticeably — Weeds are growing in the joints — Joint sand is missing or washing out — Water soaks in instead of beading on the surface — Stains from leaves, oil, or rust won't come out with regular cleaning

If you're seeing two or more of these signs, it's time.

The cleaning process

You can't seal dirty pavers. The sealant would just lock in the grime, algae, and staining underneath. So the first step — and honestly the most important step — is a thorough cleaning.

We start by pre-treating the pavers with a cleaning agent that breaks down organic growth (algae, mold, mildew) and loosens embedded dirt. Then we use a commercial surface cleaner — that's a flat spinning head with multiple spray nozzles — that delivers even, consistent pressure across the surface. This avoids the streaky, uneven look you get from a standard pressure wand.

For stubborn stains — oil in the driveway, rust from irrigation water, leaf tannin stains on the pool deck — we use targeted treatments before the main wash.

After cleaning, the pavers need to dry completely. Depending on weather, this can take 24–48 hours. Sealing wet pavers is the number one cause of sealant failure.

Joint sand replacement

This is the step most people skip — and it's a mistake. The sand between your pavers (joint sand) is what locks everything together and prevents shifting, settling, and weed growth. Over time, rain, pressure washing, and foot traffic wash this sand out.

We replace it with premium joint sand that locks into the joints, resists washout, prevents weed germination, and discourages ant colony formation. This is one of the most important factors in making your sealed pavers last.

The sealing

We apply a high quality sealer in thin, even coats using professional sprayer equipment. You'll choose between two finish options:

— Matte / natural look: enhances the paver color subtly without adding shine. Popular for driveways and walkways where you want a natural, understated appearance.

— Wet look / semi-gloss: deepens the color dramatically and adds a slight sheen. Popular for pool decks, patios, and decorative areas where you want pavers to pop.

Both provide the same protection — UV resistance, stain resistance, weed prevention, and color preservation. The difference is purely aesthetic.

What to expect after sealing

The sealant needs 24 hours to cure before foot traffic and 48 hours before vehicle traffic. During this time, you'll want to keep the surface dry.

Once cured, you'll notice an immediate difference in color depth and vibrancy. Water will bead on the surface instead of soaking in. Leaf stains and bird droppings will clean up easily instead of setting in.

Protection lasts 2–3 years depending on sun exposure, traffic, and maintenance. Driveways with heavy vehicle traffic may need resealing closer to 2 years. Pool decks and patios with less traffic may get closer to 3.

Is it worth it?

Here's how I think about it: your paver driveway and pool deck represent a significant investment. Spending a little money every 2–3 years to keep them looking new, functioning properly, and adding curb appeal to your property is one of the highest-return maintenance investments you can make.

Compare that to replacement cost if pavers deteriorate beyond repair, and the math is simple.
